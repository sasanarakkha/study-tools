# Atthi vs Hoti

Based on the suttas, there is a distinction between **atthi** and **hoti** in the Pāḷi suttas. While both can be translated as “is” or “exists,” their usage patterns suggest that **atthi** (from the root *as*) frequently denotes **immediate presence** or **direct experience**, whereas **hoti** (from the root *bhū*) often refers to **becoming**, **happening**, or **conceptual/speculative existence**.

### **1. *Atthi* and Direct Experience (The “Visible Here and Now”)**

The term *atthi* is consistently used in the context of **mindfulness (*satipaṭṭhāna*)** to describe things as they are directly experienced in the present moment.

* **Establishing Mindfulness:** In the standard instructions for contemplating the body, a monk is told to establish mindfulness with the thought: **“The body exists” (*atthi kāyo’ti*)**. Similarly, for the contemplation of phenomena: **“phenomena exist” (*atthi dhammā’ti*)**.
* **Direct Observation of Taints:** When the Buddha explains how the Dhamma is **directly visible (*sandiṭṭhiko*)**, he uses *atthi* to describe the internal recognition of mental states.
  * **Pāḷi:** *“…santaṃ vā ajjhattaṃ rāgadosamohaṃ, **atthi** me ajjhattaṃ rāgadosamohoti pajānāti”*.
  * **Translation:** “…when lust, hatred, and delusion are present internally, he knows: ‘**There is** lust, hatred, and delusion in me internally.’”
* **Present Known:** Concentration that leads to clear comprehension involves knowing feelings, perceptions, and thoughts as they **are present (*upaṭṭhahanti*)**, often accompanied by the realization of their present existence.

### **2. The *Niruttipatha Sutta*: Explicit Temporal Distinction**

A definitive source for this distinction is the *Niruttipatha Sutta*, which classifies the “paths of language” into past, future, and present.

* **Specific to the Present:** The sutta explicitly assigns *atthi* to things that have **already arisen and manifested** in the present.
  * **Pāḷi:** *“…yaṃ, bhikkhave, rūpaṃ jātaṃ pātubhūtaṃ, '**atthī**’ti tassa saṅkhā… na tassa saṅkhā 'ahosī’ti, na tassa saṅkhā 'bhavissatī’ti”*.
  * **Translation:** “…whatever form is born and manifested, it is reckoned as ‘**it is**’ (*atthi*)… it is not reckoned as ‘it was’ (*ahosi*), nor is it reckoned as ‘it will be’ (*bhavissati*).”
* **Contrast with *Hoti*:** In the same sutta, the future is described using **bhavissati** (the future form of *hoti/bhū*), signifying that which is “unborn and unmanifested”.

### **3. *Hoti* and “Coming to Be” (Dependent Origination)**

In the context of **causality**, *hoti* often functions to describe a process of **becoming** or the arising of a state dependent on conditions.

* **The Arising of Birth:** When discussing Dependent Origination, the Buddha uses *hoti* to describe the result of a condition.
  * **Pāḷi:** *“…bhave kho sati jāti **hoti**, bhavapaccayā jātī”*.
  * **Translation:** “…when becoming exists, birth **comes to be** (*hoti*); with becoming as condition, there is birth.”
* **The Mass of Suffering:** The entire process of the “origination of this whole mass of suffering” is concluded with the verb **hoti** (comes to be/arises).

### **4. *Hoti* and Speculative/Abstract Existence**

When the suttas discuss **abstract views** or speculative theories about existence (which are not directly visible here and now), the verb *hoti* is typically employed.

* **Existence After Death:** The famous undeclared questions regarding the state of a being after death use *hoti*.
  * **Pāḷi:** *“…'**hoti** tathāgato paraṃ maraṇā’ti vā, 'na **hoti** tathāgato paraṃ maraṇā’ti vā…”*.
  * **Translation:** “…‘a Tathāgata **exists** after death,’ or ‘a Tathāgata does not **exist** after death’…”
* **Metaphysical Identity:** Proclaiming a permanent self often involves *hoti* as a state of being.
  * **Pāḷi:** *“…‘Rūpī attā **hoti** arogo paraṃ maraṇā’…”*.
  * **Translation:** “…‘The self **is** material and healthy after death’…”

### **Summary of Differences**

|Feature|*Atthi* (root *as*)|*Hoti* (root *bhū*)|
| --- | --- | --- |
|**Primary Meaning**|Is, exists, is present.|Becomes, comes to be, happens.|
|**Temporal Context**|Specifically the **present**.|Often used for **future** or **generalized** states.|
|**Experiential Context**|**Direct experience**; used in mindfulness and the recognition of taints.|**Conceptual/Speculative**; used in philosophical views and causal formulas.|
|**Manifestation**|Arisen and **manifested** now.|**Arising** or **becoming** into a state.|

While *atthi* anchors the practitioner in the factual reality of what is currently present (such as “this is suffering”), *hoti* describes the dynamic process by which those realities arise or the abstract ways they are conceived by the mind.

---

Saying this, it is improtant to remember:

While *atthi* and *hoti* can have distinct meanings in certain contexts, they can also be completely interchangeable in others. This is similar to the usage of words like *mano*, *citta*, and *viññāṇa*. There are some variations in their usage, counter-examples, and instances where they are direct synonyms.
